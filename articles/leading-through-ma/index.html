Leading Through M&A: What the People Side of Mergers Actually Requires

JJ
Julian Johnson ICF PCC · ACTC Candidate · Founder, Unified Solutions Inc.
| 7 min read | April 2026

The financial and strategic analysis that precedes a major merger or acquisition is, by almost any measure, extraordinary. Investment bankers, strategic advisors, legal counsel, and due diligence teams spend months examining every material aspect of the transaction. The numbers are stress-tested. The synergies are modeled. The deal structure is optimized.

And then the deal closes, and the organization discovers whether any of that value actually materializes. The answer, consistently, is that a significant portion of it does not. Studies place M&A failure rates between 50 and 70 percent, depending on how failure is defined. The most commonly cited reason is not financial miscalculation. It is the people side.

What the people side actually means

When the failure of M&A integration is attributed to culture or people, it is frequently treated as a soft explanation for hard failures. Culture did not align. People did not adjust. The integration plan was underestimated. These explanations are accurate but imprecise, and their imprecision makes them difficult to act on.

What is actually happening in failed integrations, examined at the level of individual and team behavior, is usually more specific. The leaders who held authority in the acquired organization experience a rapid and often poorly managed shift in their actual authority, decision rights, and organizational status. The informal networks that made both organizations function, networks that live in relationships rather than org charts, are disrupted without being rebuilt. The employees of the acquired company are asked to adopt new operating norms, values, and systems while receiving mixed signals about whether the acquirer actually values what made the acquired organization distinctive.

The integration plan that exists on paper is almost never the integration that actually happens. What actually happens is determined by hundreds of decisions made by leaders under pressure, without adequate guidance, in the absence of clear authority.

What leaders in M&A actually need to navigate

I have worked in and around significant M&A events as a consultant and as a coach, and the leadership challenges that actually determine integration success fall into several consistent categories that rarely appear in integration playbooks.

The first is identity: whose organization is this now? The acquired company's leaders are typically told that this is a merger of equals, or that their culture and ways of working will be preserved, while simultaneously experiencing structural evidence to the contrary. The resulting ambiguity is not primarily a communication problem. It is a leadership credibility problem, and it cannot be solved with messaging.

The second is authority: who actually decides what now? The formal authority structure changes rapidly in an integration. The informal authority structure, the actual influence network that exists in any organization, changes much more slowly and through a process that no one is explicitly managing. Leaders who had significant informal authority in their previous organization may find that it does not transfer automatically, and that the behaviors that built it in one context are not equally effective in the new one.

The third is loss: what are we allowed to grieve? Even integrations that are objectively good for the acquired organization involve real losses, of identity, of culture, of ways of working that people valued and built over years. Organizations that do not create structured space for that loss to be acknowledged typically experience it as resistance, which then gets managed through pressure rather than through genuine engagement.

What coaching during M&A looks like

The most useful coaching intervention in an M&A context is not post-integration support. It is leadership coaching that begins in the months preceding close, when the leaders who will be responsible for integration are still forming their understanding of what they are entering and what will actually be required of them.

The questions that matter: What is your theory of how this integration succeeds? Not the organization's theory, yours. What will your personal brand be in the new organization, and how does it need to evolve? Where are you likely to move too quickly, based on your patterns under pressure? Where are you likely to move too slowly? What informal relationships do you need to build in the next 90 days, and what is your strategy for building them?

These are not questions that get asked in integration planning meetings. They require a confidential context and a structured conversation that is explicitly about the leader's experience of the transition rather than their operational responsibilities within it.
